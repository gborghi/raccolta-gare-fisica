### Task 1.A. 2012 (4 points)

One end of a rigid weightless rod is pivotally attached. To the other end, by a thread thrown over a weightless pulley, a mass $m$ is hung. Two more masses of $20m$ and $12m$ are hung by threads to the rod at the points that divide it into three equal parts (see figure). All threads are inextensible and weightless. The rod is held in a horizontal position and then released.

Find the acceleration of all the weights immediately after the release of the rod.

On a separate form you can find a graph of the current–voltage characteristic of one diode (dependence of the current through the diode on the voltage across it). Plot a graph of the current $I$ in the circuit versus the voltage source $U$, if $U$ varies from $0$ to $3\ \text{V}$.

### Task 1.C. A flat lens (3.5 points)

A round transparent plane-parallel plate of thickness $h$ is made from a material whose optical refractive index depends on the distance $r$ to the central axis of the plate according to the law

$$n(r) = n_0\!\left(1 - \beta\, r^2\right),$$

where $n_0$ and $\beta$ are known positive constants. The plate is surrounded by air with refractive index $n = 1$.

On the axis of the plate, at a distance $a$ ($a \ll h$) from it, a point light source $S$ is placed.

1. Show that the plate acts as a lens, i.e., it forms an image of the source.
2. Determine the distance from the plate at which the image of the source is formed.
3. What is the focal length $F$ of this lens?

## Problema 2







**Problem 2 — Adventures of a piston** (10 points)

An open cylindrical vessel of height $H = 30.0\ \text{cm}$ and cross-sectional area $S = 50.0\ \text{cm}^2$ is filled with air under normal conditions, i.e., at atmospheric pressure $p_0 = 1.01 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$ and temperature $T_0 = 273\ \text{K}$. A thin, heavy piston of mass $M = 50.0\ \text{kg}$ is gently inserted from the top into the vessel. The vessel walls and the piston are made of a material that conducts heat very poorly. Assume that air is an ideal diatomic gas with average molar mass $\mu = 29.0\ \text{g/mol}$; the acceleration of free fall is $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$ and the universal gas constant is $R = 8.31\ \text{J/(mol\cdot K)}$. The heat capacity of the piston and vessel, and friction on the walls, are completely ignored.

Define the dimensionless parameter

$$\alpha = \frac{Mg}{p_0 S}.$$

The piston is released. The transition to final equilibrium occurs in two stages.

**First stage:** The piston oscillates. The gas processes cannot be considered quasi-static. Because of the non-equilibrium character, the oscillations are damped and mechanical energy is dissipated. Assume that half of the dissipated energy is transferred to the gas in the vessel and the other half to the atmosphere. Thermal conductivity of the vessel and piston is negligible during this stage. After the oscillations stop, the piston comes to rest at height $H_1$.

**Second stage:** The process is slow; the piston eventually comes to rest at height $H_2$.

**2.1** [0.5 points] What is the air pressure $p_1$ in the vessel at the end of the first stage? Express the answer in terms of $p_0$, the adiabatic index $\gamma$, and $\alpha$. Find the numerical value of $p_1$.

**2.2** [1.5 points] What is the temperature $T_1$ at the end of the first stage? Express the answer in terms of $T_0$, $\gamma$, and $\alpha$. Find the numerical value of $T_1$.

**2.3** [0.5 points] Find the height $H_1$. Express the answer in terms of $H$, $\gamma$, and $\alpha$. Find the numerical value of $H_1$.

**2.4** [0.5 points] What is the air pressure $p_2$ in the vessel at the end of the second stage? Express the answer in terms of $p_0$ and $\alpha$. Find the numerical value of $p_2$.

**2.5** [0.5 points] What is the temperature $T_2$ at the end of the second stage?

**2.6** [0.5 points] Find the height $H_2$. Express the answer in terms of $H$ and $\alpha$. Find the numerical value of $H_2$.

**2.7** [2 points] Find the angular frequency $\omega$ of small oscillations of the piston about its equilibrium position, assuming the process is quasi-static and adiabatic. Express the answer in terms of $g$, $H$, $\gamma$, and $\alpha$. Find the numerical value of $\omega$.

---

At the end of the second stage, a large number of small holes are drilled in the bottom of the vessel with total area $S_0 = 5.00 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$. The size of each hole is much smaller than the mean free path of the molecules. After some time the piston moves with a constant velocity $u$.

It is known that the average number of molecules $\mathcal{N}$ hitting unit surface area per unit time is

$$\mathcal{N} = \frac{n_{\text{mol}}\,\bar{v}}{4},$$

where $\bar{v}$ is the mean thermal velocity of the molecules. The average kinetic energy of translational motion of molecules passing through the holes is

$$\langle \varepsilon_{\text{kin}} \rangle = 2\,k_B T,$$

where $k_B$ is the Boltzmann constant.

Considering that heat flow through the walls and piston is negligible, answer the following questions:

**2.8** [1 point] The final air pressure under the piston has the form $p_3 = A\,f(\alpha)$, where $A$ is a constant depending on $p_0$ and $f(\alpha)$ is a function of $\alpha$. Find $A$ and $f(\alpha)$. Find the numerical value of $p_3$.

**2.9** [2 points] The final velocity of the piston is given by $u = B\,g(\alpha)$, where $B$ is a constant depending on $S_0$, $S$, $R$, $T_0$, and $\mu$, while $g(\alpha)$ is a function of $\alpha$. Find $B$ and $g(\alpha)$. Find the numerical value of $u$.

**2.10** [1 point] The final temperature of the gas under the piston has the form $T_3 = C\,h(\alpha)$, where $C$ is a constant depending on $T_0$ and $h(\alpha)$ is a function of $\alpha$. Find $C$ and $h(\alpha)$. Find the numerical value of $T_3$.

## Problema 3







**Problem 3 — Nuclear droplet** (10 points)

In this problem we consider the main characteristics and conditions for the stability of atomic nuclei. Let an atomic nucleus contain $A$ nucleons ($A$ is the atomic mass number), namely $Z$ protons and $N = A - Z$ neutrons. The total energy of the nucleus can be written as

$$E = \left(Z m_p + N m_n - M\right)c^2 - E_p,$$

where $M$ is the mass of the nucleus, $m_p$ is the mass of the free proton, $m_n$ is the mass of the free neutron, $c$ is the speed of light, and $E_p$ is the potential energy of the nucleons in the nucleus.

The potential energy of the nucleon–nucleon interaction is described by the following semi-empirical Weizsäcker formula:

$$E_p = -a_1 A + a_2 A^{2/3} + a_3 \frac{Z^2}{A^{1/3}} + a_4 \frac{(A-2Z)^2}{A},$$

where

$$a_1 = 15.75\ \text{MeV},\quad a_2 = 17.8\ \text{MeV},\quad a_3 = 0.711\ \text{MeV},\quad a_4 = 23.7\ \text{MeV}.$$

The Weizsäcker semi-empirical formula corresponds to the liquid drop model of the atomic nucleus. The mass and charge of the nucleus are assumed to be uniformly distributed inside a sphere, and the nuclear fluid is characterised by a surface-tension parameter $\sigma$. The formula accounts for:

- **Surface energy:** accounts for the surface tension of nuclear matter in the liquid drop model;
- **Coulomb repulsion energy:** electrostatic repulsion of protons within the nucleus;
- **Symmetry (exchange) energy:** reflects the tendency towards stability at $N = Z$;
- **Volume energy:** direct dependence on the number of nucleons $A$ due to nuclear forces.

In deriving this formula, Weizsäcker used the experimentally established dependence of the nuclear radius on the number of nucleons:

$$R(A) = R_0\, A^{1/3},$$

where $R_0$ is a constant.

Based on the above, answer the following questions:

**3.1** [2 points] Find the electrostatic energy $E_C$ of a uniformly charged sphere of radius $R$ with total charge $Q$. Express the answer in terms of $Q$, the permittivity of free space $\varepsilon_0$, and $R$.

**3.2** [1 point] Find the numerical value of the constant $R_0$ in the formula $R(A) = R_0\,A^{1/3}$.

**3.3** [1 point] Find the numerical value of the density $\rho_m$ of nuclear matter.

**3.4** [1 point] Find the numerical value of the surface tension $\sigma$ of nuclear matter (the analogue of surface tension for the nuclear liquid).

---

Suppose now that the nucleus splits into two fragments with atomic mass numbers $kA$ and $(1-k)A$ respectively, where $0 < k < 1$. The nuclear charge and number of neutrons are distributed between the fragments in proportion to the atomic mass number.

**3.5** [2 points] Nuclear fission becomes energetically favourable under the condition $Z^2/A > f(k)$. Find an expression for the function $f(k)$ and sketch it schematically.

**3.6** [0.5 points] With accuracy to two significant figures, find the limiting value $(Z^2/A)_0$ at which spontaneous fission is theoretically possible.

---

Under the condition of problem 3.5, the nucleus can remain intact for a very long time (e.g., the half-life of $^{235}\text{U}$ is 713 million years). Instantaneous fission is prevented by an energy barrier that disappears at a critical value. In practice the nucleus breaks when a significant deviation from spherical shape occurs.

For simplicity, assume that the nucleus undergoes deformations in which its surface becomes a prolate ellipsoid of revolution described in Cartesian coordinates by

$$\frac{x^2 + y^2}{a^2} + \frac{z^2}{b^2} = 1,$$

where $a$ is the small (equatorial) semi-axis and $b$ is the large (polar) semi-axis.

The volume of a prolate spheroid is:

$$V = \frac{4}{3}\pi a^2 b,$$

and its surface area is:

$$\Sigma = 2\pi a^2 \left(1 + \frac{b}{a\,e}\arcsin e\right), \qquad e = \sqrt{1 - \frac{a^2}{b^2}}.$$

Let the spherical nucleus undergo a deformation such that $b = R(1+\varepsilon)$ and $a = R(1-\lambda)$, with $\varepsilon,\, \lambda \ll 1$ and $R$ the initial radius of the nuclear droplet.

**3.7** [0.5 points] Find the relation between $\varepsilon$ and $\lambda$ (use conservation of nuclear volume).

It can be shown that the electrostatic energy of the protons in the deformed nucleus is approximately:

$$E_C^{\text{deformed}} = E_C\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{5}\varepsilon^2\right).$$

**3.8** [2 points] Find the expression and the numerical value of $(Z^2/A)_{\text{critical}}$.

---

*Known physical constants:*
- Elementary charge: $e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$
- Permittivity of free space: $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{F/m}$
- Mass of nucleon (proton or neutron): $m_N \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$
- $1\ \text{eV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J}$
