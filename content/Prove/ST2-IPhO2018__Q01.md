# OII 2018 Teorica — Problema 1

Solutions to Theory Problem 2
Where is the neutrino?
(Miguel C N Fiolhais and António Onofre)
July 24, 2018
v1.2

Where is the neutrino? (10 points)

Part A. ATLAS Detector physics (4.0 points)

**A.1**

The magnetic force is the centripetal force:

$$m\frac{v^2}{r}= evB\Rightarrow r= \frac{mv}{eB}.$$

First express the velocity in terms of the kinetic energy,

$$K= \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \Rightarrow v= \sqrt{\frac{2K}{m}},$$

and then insert it in the expression above for the radius to get

A.1: $\displaystyle r= \frac{\sqrt{2Km}}{eB}.$ (0.5pt)

**A.2**

The radius of the circular motion of a charged particle in the presence of a uniform magnetic field is given by,

$$r= \frac{mv}{eB}.$$

This formula is valid in the relativistic scenario if the mass correction, $m\to\gamma m$ is included:

$$r= \frac{\gamma mv}{eB}= \frac{p}{eB}\Rightarrow p= reB.$$

Note that the radius of the circular motion is half the radius of the inner part of the detector. One obtains $[1\ \text{MeV}/c= 5.34 \times 10^{-22}\ \text{m kg s}^{-1}]$

A.2: $p= 330\ \text{MeV}/c.$ (0.5pt)

**A.3**

The acceleration for the particle is $a= \frac{evB}{\gamma m}\sim\frac{ecB}{\gamma m}$, in the ultrarelativistic limit. Then,

$$P= \frac{e^4 c^2\gamma^4 B^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3\gamma^2 m^2} = \frac{e^4\gamma^2 c^4 B^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^5 m^2}.$$

Since $E= \gamma mc^2$ we can obtain $\gamma^2 c^4 = \dfrac{E^2}{m^2}$ and, finally,

$$P= \frac{e^4}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 m^4 c^5}\, E^2 B^2.$$

Therefore,

A.3: $\xi= \dfrac{1}{6\pi},\quad n= 5\ \text{and}\ k= 4.$ (1.0pt)

**A.4**

The power emitted by the particle is given by,

$$P= -\frac{dE}{dt}= \frac{e^4}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 m^4 c^5}\, E^2 B^2 .$$

The energy of the particle as a function of time can be calculated from

$$\int_{E_0}^{E(t)} \frac{1}{E^2}\, dE= -\int_0^t \frac{e^4}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 m^4 c^5}\, B^2\, dt,$$

where $E(0) = E_0$. This leads to,

$$\frac{1}{E(t)} -\frac{1}{E_0} = \frac{e^4 B^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 m^4 c^5}\, t \Rightarrow E(t) = \frac{E_0}{1 + \alpha E_0 t},$$

with

A.4: $\alpha= \dfrac{e^4 B^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 m^4 c^5}.$ (1.0pt)

**A.5**

If the initial energy of the electron is 100 GeV, the radius of curvature is extremely large ($r= \frac{E}{eBc}\approx 167\ \text{m}$). Therefore, in approximation, one can consider the electron is moving in the inner part of the ATLAS detector along a straight line. The time of flight of the electron is $t= R/c$, where $R= 1.1\ \text{m}$ is the radius of the inner part of the detector. The total energy lost due to synchrotron radiation is,

$$\Delta E= E(R/c) -E_0 = \frac{E_0}{1 + \alpha E_0 \frac{R}{c}} -E_0 \approx -\alpha E_0^2 \frac{R}{c}$$

and

A.5: $\Delta E= -56\ \text{MeV} .$ (0.5pt)

**A.6**

In the ultrarelativistic limit, $v\approx c$ and $E\approx pc$. The cyclotron frequency is,

$$\omega(t) = \frac{c}{r(t)} = \frac{ecB}{p(t)} = \frac{ec^2 B}{E(t)}$$

A.6: $\omega(t) = \dfrac{ec^2 B}{E_0}\left(1 + \dfrac{e^4 B^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 m^4 c^5}\, E_0 t\right) .$ (0.5pt)

Part B. Finding the neutrino (6.0 points)

**B.1**

Since the $W^+$ boson decays into an anti-muon and a neutrino, one can use principles of conservation of energy and linear momentum to calculate the unknown $p^{(\nu)}_z$ of the neutrino. Moreover, the anti-muon and the neutrino can be considered massless, which implies that the magnitude of their momenta (times $c$) and their energies are the same. Therefore, the conservation of linear momentum can be expressed as

$$\vec{p}^{\,(W)} = \vec{p}^{\,(\mu)} + \vec{p}^{\,(\nu)} ,$$

and the conservation of energy as,

$$E^{(W)} = cp^{(\mu)} + cp^{(\nu)} .$$

In addition, one can also relate the energy and the momentum of the $W^+$ boson through its mass,

$$m_W^2= (E^{(W)})^2/c^4 -(p^{(W)})^2/c^2$$

which leads to a quadratic equation on $p^{(\nu)}_z$,

$$m_W^2 = \left[(p^{(\mu)} + p^{(\nu)})^2 -(\vec{p}^{\,(\mu)} + \vec{p}^{\,(\nu)})^2\right] /c^2 = \left(2p^{(\mu)}p^{(\nu)} -2\, \vec{p}^{\,(\mu)} \cdot\vec{p}^{\,(\nu)}\right) /c^2$$

B.1:
$$m_W^2= \frac{1}{c^2} \left(2p^{(\mu)}\sqrt{(p^{(\nu)}_T)^2 + (p^{(\nu)}_z)^2} -2\, \vec{p}^{\,(\mu)}_T \cdot\vec{p}^{\,(\nu)}_T -2p^{(\mu)}_z p^{(\nu)}_z\right) .$$
(1.5pt)

**B.2**

The numerical substitution directly in the answer of B.1, using

$$p^{(\mu)} = 37.2\ \text{GeV}/c \qquad m_W^2 c^2 = 6464.2\ (\text{GeV}/c)^2 \qquad p^{(\nu)\,2}_T = 10\,864.9\ (\text{GeV}/c)^2$$

$$\vec{p}^{\,(\mu)}_T \cdot\vec{p}^{\,(\nu)}_T = 2439.3\ (\text{GeV}/c)^2 \qquad p^{(\mu)}_z = -12.4\ \text{GeV}/c ,$$

leads to

$$6464.2 = 74.4\sqrt{10\,864.9 + p^{(\nu)\,2}_z} -4878.6 + 24.8\, p^{(\nu)} .$$

This is a quadratic equation, equivalent to

$$0.88889\, p^{(\nu)\,2}_z + 101.64\, p^{(\nu)}_z -12378 = 0$$

whose solutions are:

B.2: $p^{(\nu)}_z = 74.0\ \text{GeV}/c$ or $p^{(\nu)}_z = -188.3\ \text{GeV}/c.$ (1.5pt)

The general solution of the equation above in B.1 leads to

$$p^{(\nu)}_z = \frac{2\, \vec{p}^{\,(\mu)}_T \cdot\vec{p}^{\,(\nu)}_T\, p^{(\mu)}_z + m_W^2 c^2 p^{(\mu)}_z}{2(p^{(\mu)}_T)^2} \pm \frac{p^{(\mu)} \sqrt{-4(p^{(\mu)}_T)^2(p^{(\nu)}_T)^2 + 4(\vec{p}^{\,(\mu)}_T \cdot\vec{p}^{\,(\nu)}_T)^2 + 4\, \vec{p}^{\,(\mu)}_T \cdot\vec{p}^{\,(\nu)}_T\, m_W^2 c^2 + m_W^4 c^4}}{2(p^{(\mu)}_T)^2}$$

Numerical substitution leads to the above mentioned values for $p^{(\nu)}_z$.

**B.3**

The final state particles of the top quark decay are the anti-muon, the neutrino and jet 1. Since the neutrino is now fully reconstructed the energy and linear momentum of the top quark can be calculated as,

$$E^{(t)} = cp^{(\mu)} + cp^{(\nu)} + cp^{(j_1)}$$
$$\vec{p}^{\,(t)} = \vec{p}^{\,(\mu)} + \vec{p}^{\,(\nu)} + \vec{p}^{\,(j_1)} .$$

The top quark mass is,

$$m_t = \sqrt{(E^{(t)})^2/c^4 -(\vec{p}^{\,(t)})^2/c^2} = c^{-1}\sqrt{(p^{(\mu)} + p^{(\nu)} + p^{(j_1)})^2 -(\vec{p}^{\,(\mu)} + \vec{p}^{\,(\nu)} + \vec{p}^{\,(j_1)})^2} .$$

The substitution of values leads to two possible masses:

B.3: $m_t = 169.3\ \text{GeV}/c^2$ or $m_t = 311.2\ \text{GeV}/c^2$ (1.0pt)

**B.4**

According to the frequency distribution for signal (dashed line), the probability of the $m_t = 169.3\ \text{GeV}/c^2$ solution is roughly 0.1 while the probability of the $m_t = 311.2\ \text{GeV}/c^2$ solution is below 0.01. Therefore,

B.4: The most likely candidate is the $m_t = 169.3\ \text{GeV}/c^2$ solution. (1.0pt)

**B.5**

The top quark energy for the most likely candidate is $E^{(t)} = cp^{(\mu)} + cp^{(\nu)} + cp^{(j_1)} = 272.6\ \text{GeV}$ .

$$d= vt= v\gamma t_0 = \frac{p^{(t)}}{m_t}\, t_0 = ct_0\sqrt{\frac{E^{(t)\,2}}{m_t^2 c^4} -1} .$$

B.5: $d= 2 \times 10^{-16}\ \text{m} .$ (1.0pt)
