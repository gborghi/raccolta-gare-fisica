Solution 1
1. So first integral is equation 6. To do that we just need to substitute $u^2 = \dfrac{\alpha v^2}{2}$
and $du = dv\sqrt{\dfrac{\alpha}{2}}$. So integral becomes

$$C\sqrt{\frac{2}{\alpha}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-u^2}\,du = 1.$$

And this is Gaussian integral which is equals to $\sqrt{\pi}$. So constant is

$$\sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{2\pi}}$$

2. Second integral is equation 9.

$$\langle v^2\rangle = \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} v_x^2 \exp\left(-\frac{\alpha v_x^2}{2}\right) dv_x$$

This can be doable using integration by parts. So we want to differentiate $v_x$ and
integrate other part

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
 & D & I \\
+ & v_x & v_x e^{-\frac{\alpha v_x^2}{2}} \\
- & 1 & -\frac{1}{\alpha} e^{-\frac{\alpha v_x^2}{2}}
\end{array}
$$

So integral becomes

$$-\frac{v_x}{2\alpha} e^{-\frac{\alpha v_x^2}{2}}\Bigg|_{-\infty}^{\infty} + \frac{1}{2\alpha}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{\alpha v_x^2}{2}}\,dv_x$$

First term vanishes because of exponential. Second term is same as integral 1. So
overall doing same steps we get

$$\langle v_x^2\rangle = \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{2\pi}}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\alpha^3}} = \frac{1}{\alpha}$$

3. Third integral is equation 10. We can use same method for this.but this time we
will different $v^2$. So

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
 & D & I \\
+ & v^2 & v\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right) \\
- & 2v & -\frac{kT}{m}\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right)
\end{array}
$$

The same as integral two first term vanishes because of exponential and we left
with

$$\int_0^\infty \frac{kT}{m}\,2v\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{kT}\right) dv$$

So if we substitute $u = \dfrac{mv^2}{kT}$ we get

$$\int_0^\infty 2\left(\frac{kT}{m}\right)^2 e^{-u}\,du = 2\left(\frac{kT}{m}\right)^2$$

Overall

$$\langle v\rangle = 4\pi\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2} \cdot 2\left(\frac{kT}{m}\right)^2 = \sqrt{\frac{8kT}{\pi m}}$$

Solution 2
For this problem we just need to find $\left\langle \dfrac{1}{v}\right\rangle$
So by the definition

$$\left\langle \frac{1}{v}\right\rangle = \int_0^\infty \frac{f(v)}{v}\,dv = 4\pi\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\int_0^\infty v\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right) dv$$

using provided integral 12 we get that

$$\left\langle \frac{1}{v}\right\rangle = 4\pi\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\frac{kT}{m} = \sqrt{\frac{2m}{\pi kT}}$$

And $\langle v\rangle = \sqrt{\dfrac{8kT}{\pi m}}$ is known so if we multiply them we get $\dfrac{4}{\pi}$

Solution 3
Original Maxwell's distribution is

$$f(v)\,dv = 4\pi v^2\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right) dv$$

For transformation we must have

$$F(E)\,dE = f(v)\,dv \implies F(E)\,dE = f(v)\,\frac{dv}{dE}\,dE$$

We can find its derivative by the definition $E = \dfrac{mv^2}{2} \implies \dfrac{dE}{dv} = mv$ So $F(E)\,dE$ is

$$F(E)\,dE = 4\pi v^2\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right)\left(\frac{1}{mv}\right) dE = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}(kT)^{3/2}}\sqrt{E}\exp\left(-\frac{E}{kT}\right) dE$$

Or

$$a = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}(kT)^{3/2}} \qquad b = \frac{1}{2}$$

Solution 4
New deBroglie wavelength distribution should satisfy the condition

$$f(v)\,dv = -F(\lambda)\,d\lambda \implies -f(v)\,\frac{dv}{d\lambda}\,d\lambda$$

By definition $\dfrac{d\lambda}{dv} = -\dfrac{h}{mv^2}$ Combining equations and replacing parameter $v = \dfrac{h}{m\lambda}$ yields

$$F(\lambda)\,d\lambda = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\frac{h^3}{\lambda^4 (mkT)^{3/2}}\exp\left(-\frac{h^2}{2mkT\lambda^2}\right) d\lambda$$

or

$$a = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\frac{h^3}{(mkT)^{3/2}} \qquad b = 4 \qquad c = \frac{h^2}{2mkT}$$

Solution 5
Like hint says lets first look at simplified problem. Lets assume all the molecules from
origin move with velocity $v$ in cylindrical shape and to one direction $\theta$ with the normal
of the wall
Stage 1
Then during time interval $\Delta t$ only molecules with height $v\Delta t$ can strike to the wall.The
number of molecules can strike will be

$$N_1 = n\Delta V = nv\Delta t\,S\cos\theta$$

Where $S\cos\theta$ is the cross sectional area of that beam.
Stage 2
Now lets average this with Maxwell's distribution. The range of velocity is $[0;\infty]$ the
polar angle $[0;2\pi]$ the azimithual angle is $\left[0;\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right]$. Now

$$N_c = \sum nSv\cos\theta\,\Delta t\left(v^2\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right)\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi\,dv\right)$$

$$= nS\Delta t\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\int_0^\infty v^3\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right) dv\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\theta\sin\theta\,d\theta\int_0^{2\pi} d\phi$$

Using provided integral 14

$$N_c = nS\Delta t\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\cdot\left(\frac{1}{2}\frac{(2kT)^2}{m^2}\right)\frac{1}{2}\cdot 2\pi$$

So canceling out terms we get an accurate estimation for number of collisions for ideal
gas with temperature $T$ is

$$N_c = n\sqrt{\frac{kT}{2\pi m}}S\Delta t$$

Solution 6
So we can consider the scenario in the figure 4 for simplified problem. So we know that
the pressure will be $P = 2mnv^2\cos^2\theta$ from proof the lemma 1.
Let's average it with Maxwell's distribution.

$$P = \sum 2mnv^2\cos^2\theta\cdot\left(v^2\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right)\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi\,dv\right)$$

$$= 2mn\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\int_0^\infty v^4\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right) dv\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^2\theta\sin\theta\,d\theta\int_0^{2\pi} d\phi$$

Here we can use the integral 15 and get the result:

$$P = 2mn\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\cdot\left(\frac{3\sqrt{\pi}}{8}\left(\frac{5kT}{m}\right)^{5/2}\right)\cdot\frac{1}{3}2\pi = nkT$$

Solution 7
Average kinetic energy inside of container is

$$\langle E_\text{inside}\rangle = \frac{3}{2}kT$$

according to lemma 1 or you can calculate it by

$$\langle E_\text{inside}\rangle = \int_0^\infty \frac{mv^2}{2}f(v)\,dv$$

For the outside of vessel we can calculate it like other problems.
Consider a simple
scenario with all the molecules with the same velocity they are mocing in same direction.
In this case the number of molecules that go outside will be

$$N_\text{out} = nvS\cos\theta\,\Delta t$$

Now let's average this energy and number of molecules with Maxwell's distribution. We
have derived number of molecules before. It is

$$N_\text{out} = n\sqrt{\frac{kT}{2\pi m}}S\Delta t$$

So applying similar approach for $E_\text{out}$

$$E_\text{out} = \sum \frac{mv^2}{2}nvS\cos\theta\,\Delta t\left(v^2\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right)\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi\,dv\right)$$

$$= \frac{mnS\Delta t}{2}\left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}\int_0^\infty v^5\exp\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2kT}\right) dv\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\theta\sin\theta\,d\theta\int_0^{2\pi} d\phi$$

Using the integral 16 yields

$$E_\text{out} = nS\Delta t\,2kT\sqrt{\frac{kT}{2\pi m}}$$

The avarage energy of molecules in the outside will be

$$\langle E_\text{outside}\rangle = \frac{E_\text{out}}{N_\text{out}}$$

So dividing energy to number of molecules we get

$$\langle E_\text{out}\rangle = 2kT$$

Our final result is

$$\frac{\langle E_\text{outside}\rangle}{\langle E_\text{inside}\rangle} = \frac{4}{3}$$
